Part I: Dispossession Theory

Understanding Recursive Dispossession

Theoretical Premise

Recursive Dispossession

The foundationalist presupposition of theories of dispossession operates simultaneously on two levels: on the level of subjectivity, or a subject’s relationship to themself, and on the level of a subject’s relationship to land. In the first instance, subjects are presumed to own themselves, their bodies, or their labor before dispossession, and thus to seek the return of those stolen elements of their selves. In the second instance, land is presumably possessed by individuals or by collectivities, either by right or custom, as property or as commons, such that anti dispossessive politics ought to seek the return of that land to the individuals or collectivities from which it was stolen. However, these presuppositions often do not specify in what sense subjects can be said to have previously owned themselves or their land, and thus fail to consider the political implications of imputing prior possession; as Marx put in the passage on Proudhon: “since ‘theft’ as a forcible violation of property presupposes the existence of property” 12. The “theft” framework however, presumes prior possession--- it obscures the ante-possessive social relations that preceded and opposed dispossession and it restricts redress to juridical disputes over who should be recognized as a proper possessor.

Part II: The Adam Saffin Case

New England

Figure 2.1: Map of Plymouth County, 1620–1691.
An Overview

Dispossession in colonial New England

The legal struggles of Adam, an enslaved man in late seventeenth-century Massachusetts, highlight an intricate genealogy of how possession, bondage, and freedom were negotiated under the evolving rules of colonial law. His experiences offer a direct critique of the static framework of 'prior possession,' exposing instead a dynamic arena where enslaved individuals utilized institutional frameworks to reconfigure their localized social relationships.

Rather than merely relying on abstract claims to self-ownership, Adam challenged his subjugation by navigating existing contractual arrangements, municipal agreements, and regional networks of community support. His case demonstrates how the dispossessed worked within and against colonial structures to contest arbitrary bounds of ownership, reshaping their ongoing operational status relative to their masters and peers alike.

In 1701, the structural parameters of this tension came to a head in the Massachusetts courts through the litigation surrounding *Adam v. John Saffin*. The legal proceedings focused heavily on the enforcement of a conditional manumission agreement, revealing how colonial spaces tried to quantify human liberty as property. By examining Adam's proactive navigation of the judicial apparatus, we find a rich historical lineage of anti-dispossessive politics operating right from within the heart of early New England.

Historical Context

Indigenous New England

Bristol and Swansey were carved from Wampanoag, Narragansett, Nipmuc, Massachusett, and Abenaki homelands, not empty ground. No event shaped the balance of power between these nations and English colonists more than King Philip's War of 1675–1676, when a coalition led by the Wampanoag sachem Metacom fought the New England Confederation. Roughly forty percent of the region's Native population was killed or driven out, and thousands of survivors were enslaved or indentured.

The war's aftermath hardened the same colonial legal authority Adam later confronted: English courts claimed sweeping new power over Native land and Native lives, compelling many into "praying towns" under colonial jurisdiction. Indigenous dispossession and the dispossession of enslaved people were interlocking processes built on the same claims to control land, labor, and bodies, even as Indigenous nations sustained their own literacies, such as the Abenaki tradition of "awikhigan," writing in birchbark, maps, and letters, as an enduring counterweight to that erasure.

Historical Context

Colonial New England's Legal System

The courts that later decided Adam's fate did not emerge from a neutral or fully formed body of law. Colonial New England's legal system was a patchwork built from English common law, Puritan religious doctrine, and locally improvised statutes designed to govern land, labor, and community order in a settler colony. Magistrates were typically prominent laymen rather than trained jurists, and colonies like Massachusetts Bay and Plymouth operated their own courts with considerable independence from England, at least until royal oversight tightened in the late seventeenth century.

Slavery existed within this system without a single, settled legal definition. Massachusetts became the first English colony to formally sanction slavery in its 1641 Body of Liberties, yet the boundaries between indentured servitude, conditional bondage, and lifetime enslavement remained genuinely contested and were fought out case by case, in exactly the kind of dispute that would define Adam's suit against Saffin decades later. Courts were simultaneously instruments of the propertied class, as Saffin's manipulation of his own case makes clear, and sites that enslaved and dispossessed people could, and did, use to press claims of their own.

Background

Adam and John Saffin

Before their dispute reached the courts, Adam and John Saffin were bound together in the ordinary, asymmetrical way that defined enslaver and enslaved in colonial New England. Saffin was a wealthy Boston merchant, landowner, and judge, a man accustomed to having his authority over his property, human and otherwise, go unquestioned. Adam was one of the people whose labor built that wealth, hired out across Saffin's various holdings in Bristol, Swansey, and Boston for years at a time, with little say over where he worked or for whom.

Their relationship was never simply that of master and laborer, however. Saffin's own writings betray a man who saw Adam's assertions of will, his refusals, his complaints, his eventual claim to freedom, as personal affronts rather than legitimate grievances, describing him in his private correspondence as "proud," "surlie," and "rascally" for behavior that amounted to Adam expecting the terms of his own bondage to be honored. That friction, over who controlled the conditions of Adam's labor and what, if anything, Saffin owed him in return, is what eventually hardened into the legal conflict examined below.

The Story

Adam v. John Saffin

John Saffin enslaved Adam sometime before 1694, when he hired Adam out to farmer Thomas Shepherd Jr. for seven years alongside the Boundfield farm on the Bristol–Swansey border. Adam later claimed the 1694 deed governing this arrangement promised him freedom at the end of the term; Saffin claimed Adam had forfeited that promise by failing to work "chearfully quietly and Industriously" as the indenture required.

By 1699, with his term nearly up, Adam was back under Saffin's direct control in Boston, hired out piecemeal for his keep. When Saffin tried to send him to work in Bristol again, Adam refused and left on his own terms, convinced he was already free. He turned to Samuel Sewall and Isaac Addington, prominent Bostonians who ordered Saffin to release him, a demand Saffin dismissed as a "Negromantick Summons" before initiating a lawsuit against Adam.

Saffin rigged the first hearing in 1701, seating himself as judge and stacking the jury, and the court ruled against Adam. Sewall, Addington, and Governor Thomas Dudley subsequently removed Saffin from the bench for this misconduct and helped Adam appeal. The case dragged on through a bout of smallpox, repeated attempts by Saffin to have Adam sent out of the colony, and a second unfavorable ruling in 1703, before the court finally decided in Adam's favor that November. Adam remained a free man until his death sometime after August 1715.

Aftermath

Sewall and Saffin's Pamphlet Dispute

The courtroom fight over Adam's freedom spilled into print. In 1700, while the case was still unresolved, Samuel Sewall published The Selling of Joseph: A Memorial, a short pamphlet arguing against slavery on religious and moral grounds and widely regarded as one of the earliest public calls for emancipation in Anglo-American history. Though the pamphlet never names Adam or Saffin directly, its timing and arguments were unmistakably shaped by the dispute unfolding between them.

Saffin responded in 1701 with A Brief and Candid Answer to a Late Printed Sheet, Entitled, The Selling of Joseph, defending slavery as biblically and socially justified and casting Sewall's position as naive interference with the natural order of servitude. The exchange made the two men's private legal battle into a early public argument over the legitimacy of slavery itself, with Adam's case as its immediate, if unspoken, occasion.

Analysis

Implications

Throughout his extensive legal battle against John Saffin, Adam demonstrated that his resistance was fundamentally driven by an effort to enforce, preserve, and reshape his social and legal standing within colonial society, rather than merely engaging in a simple transaction for the abstract ideal of property ownership.

Adam's legal maneuvering functions as an ante-possessive challenge because it destabilizes the dynamic of accumulation itself. His defense relied on proving that the terms of his bondage had been legally dissolved by an explicit covenant—a structural claim that repositioned his body entirely outside of Saffin's right of capital accumulation. By utilizing the colony’s own legal codes against his enslaver, Adam forced the courts to acknowledge the limits of racialized property rights within the institutional frameworks of New England.

Centuries later, the structural components highlighted by Adam's suit continue to expose how systems of accumulation operate by dispossessing individuals under modern juridical frameworks. For instance, contemporary contract labor systems and corporate property disputes frequently demand that human protections be parsed out strictly in terms of individualized losses, obscuring broader communal and social relations.

By shifting our perspective to view these historical records through an ante-possessive lens, we uncover how localized communities and marginalized figures continue to construct tactical networks of mutual aid and legal pushback inside hostile territory, asserting their autonomy through systemic frameworks without surrendering to them.

Part III: The Juan Patricio Case

Yucatán Peninsula

An Overview

Dispossession in the Yucatán Peninsula

Juan Patricio's story demonstrates a genealogy of how possession and dispossession were and are apprehended and lived, which is to say accounts that unsettle the values we presumptively impute to possession and dispossession, including the values we impute to struggles against dispossession.

In fact, the dispossessed did not always challenge their dispossession by claiming prior possession (of their bodies, their lands, their labor) or by demanding renewed possession as a singular remedy. In many cases, the dispossessed placed a much higher value on shaping and maintaining their social relations both with other dispossessed people and with the dispossessors they daily confront than they do in gaining or regaining possessions like land, land, or even their own bodies.

Thus, highlighting differences among cultures in defining what is "possessed" and the value placed on those "possessions" In 1696, a case in Yucatán was presented: “Certified copy of criminal proceedings brought by Doña Isabel Solis, resident of Yucatán, against Juan Patricio slave, for having beaten Don Ignacio de Esquivel, priest.” In this story, Juan Patricio and Fabiana Pech help us to chart a history of the not-yet-present of our own antidispossessive politics.

Transatlantic Slave Trade

The Middle Passage

Less than two centuries ago, the idea of shipping humans across the Atlantic was as ordinary as the trade of textiles and coffee. The transatlantic slave trade, which lasted from the 16th to 19th century, saw between 10 (conservative estimate) and 17 (generous estimate) million enslaved Africans forcibly transported across the Atlantic Ocean to the Americas. It is estimated that 15 to 25 percent of those who were transported died aboard slave ships.

One of the most critical points of contact between Europeans and Africans for centuries, the trade saw the shipment of enslaved Africans begin steadily, with around a few hundred thousand Africans making the journey prior to the 17th century. As plantations in the Caribbean and Chesapeake areas emerged, however, demand for enslaved labor rose. In the 18th century, nearly three-fifths of the total volume of the trade was transported from Africa to the Americas.

The journey, often referred to as the Middle Passage, was a brutal, unsanitary trip. Lasting several weeks to months, Africans were packed tightly in rows below decks, suffering sexual abuse, intolerable heat, low oxygen levels, and rampant disease. At one point, Captain Luke Collingwood of slave ship Zong ordered that more than 130 Africans be thrown overboard to prevent the spread of disease. He later filed an insurance claim on the value of those murdered.

These atrocities did not begin with trips between the Old and New Worlds. African traders, engaged in the act of capturing Africans to sell into slavery, often exchanged captives for commodities from Asia. This trade enabled the sale of human lives in exchange for luxury goods, firearms, liquor, cowrie shells, textiles.

The transatlantic slave trade was not a one-step mechanism. It involved the dispossession of African men, women, and children at multiple levels, from victimization at the hands of African slavers, to abuse by Europeans who profited off the trade, to degradation by Americans who relied on slave labor to generate their wealth.

Juan Patricio in the 1600s would have had his life fundamentally shaped by the Transatlantic Slave Trade.

Implications

Throughout the duration of the conflict, Juan Patricio shows himself to be much less interested in gaining or regaining any possessions he putatively lost, than he is in actively shaping the social relations he has with Black and Indigenous people, as well as both Creoles and Spaniards.

Juan Patricio's challenge to dispossession is thus ante-possessive, in that it is at once before, against, and opposite possession itself. Before possession, because he acts in a world that has not yet been entirely subsumed under racial capital's form of value; against possession, because he opposed Esquivel's attempt to possess the likes of Fabianna Pech and himself; and opposite possession, because he asserts his active role in social relations among Afro-Yucatecans, Maya, and Creoles alongside the dispossessor's ongoing power of accumulation.

More than half a decade later, Juan Patricio's case demonstrates that racial capitalism continues to be an ongoing process of accumulation by dispossession in the Americas.

For instance, public discourse about migration to the US continues to describe a system of accumulation by dispossession in individualistic terms. Individuals from Central America and Mexico are presumed to migrate, first, because they lost possession over something they previously owned—such as their labor, their land, their autonomy, their safety—and second, because they want to regain the possessions they lost. This no doubt can be said to be true for some people, yet, at least some migrants seem to have migrated for other reasons and seem to want something entirely different from such possessions.

Migration itself, including the often maligned “caravans” of migrants moving from Central America to the US, can be understood as a kind of improvised community, an effort to configure new social relations in the face of threats to or the veritable, the destruction of prior social relations.

Learning how contemporary migrants come to the US in the interest of and with the desire to protect, restore, or forge new social relations that have been stressed or even destroyed by the relentless logics of dispossession, and then are required to narrate those interests and desires as lost individual possessions, can teach us how to approach dispossession for traces of ante-possession. From seventeenth-century villages like Tahmek to twenty-first-century detention centers in El Paso, varied, vigorous challenges to accumulation by dispossession thrive, refusing possession itself in the name of lives otherwise lived.
